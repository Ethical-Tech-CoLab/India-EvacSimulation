# Peer Review — The Evacuation Readiness and Uncertainty Simulator (ERUS): A Research Report on a Teaching Instrument for Showing How Poor Field Information Degrades Civilian Evacuation Decisions

**Reviewer role:** External referee, reviewing as a tool/systems paper supporting doctoral thesis research (the bar the report sets for itself in §13.1: legibility to thesis examiners and stakeholders, plus teaching use).
**Recommendation:** Major revisions
**Date:** 22 July 2026

Note on references: per the delivery constraints of this toolchain, source citations are collected in a `## References` section and marked inline as [1], [2], etc., rather than as Word footnotes.

---

## Summary of the submission

ERUS is a single-page, dependency-free browser tool that generates a fully synthetic humanitarian-evacuation scenario — a set of candidate destinations, each scored on seven factors, and a set of evacuee groups — and then assigns groups to destinations under explicitly modelled information uncertainty. The report is a plain-language research write-up of that instrument: what it computes, why each parameter is set where it is, and what the tool is and is not for.

Read generously, the contribution is genuinely thoughtful and sits on three legs. First, an **architectural separation of ground truth from knowability**: the scenario is generated once and never re-generated when the Field Uncertainty slider moves (§4.2), so any change in predicted outcomes is attributable to information quality alone. Second, **two epistemic distinctions that most scoring systems flatten** (§7): a confident admission of ignorance versus an untrustworthy one (falling out of the confidence adjustment in §5.4), and an open question (Unknown) versus a settled refusal (Unwilling), the latter deliberately relabelled so the tool cannot present a refusal as a closable intelligence gap (§7.2, §14.2). Third, **exact reproducibility**: every output is determined by four numbers encoded in the URL (§9), so any claim can be regenerated and checked in seconds. The report is also unusually candid about the instrument's immaturity (§11–§12): no calibration, no tests, no licence, and an authoritative methodology document that is missing from the repository. This is careful, self-aware work, and the design reasoning — especially the gatekeeper cap (§5.5) and the promotion of Willingness to gatekeeper status (§5.1, §14.2) — is the strongest part of the submission.

I verified the core mechanics against the repository source (`index.html`), and the report describes the implemented formulas accurately. My concerns are therefore not about correctness of description but about what the report *claims to demonstrate* and the evidentiary basis it rests on.

## Major issues

**1. The headline "result" is true by construction, but the report presents it as a demonstration.** *Location: §1.3, §3.1, §6.5, §14.1 (framing); §5.3–§5.4 and §4.4 (mechanism).* The report's central and repeated claim is that "the same underlying reality, assessed under worse information, produces materially worse predicted outcomes" (§1.3), and §14.1 calls the monotonic decline of the sensitivity curve "a negative result it makes easy to see." But this is not a result the tool could ever fail to produce. In the code, uncertainty enters *only* through `effectiveConf = baseConf × (1 − fieldUncertainty)`, and a factor's score is `base × (0.5 + 0.5 × effectiveConf)` — monotonically increasing in effective confidence. Raising uncertainty therefore lowers every non-Blocked factor's score deterministically, which lowers mean readiness, which pushes more Monte Carlo trials below the 40% success floor (§5.10). Perturbation probability, `0.85 × (1 − effectiveConf)`, likewise rises with uncertainty, adding variance near the threshold. The downward sensitivity curve is thus an algebraic identity of the scoring rule, not an emergent finding. *Why it matters:* an examiner who notices this will read the paper's principal empirical claim as circular — the tool assumes uncertainty degrades outcomes and then "discovers" exactly that. *Path forward:* reframe the contribution honestly. The tool does not *demonstrate that* uncertainty degrades decisions; it *operationalises and makes legible* an assumed relationship and lets a user inspect its shape and magnitude under stated parameters. State plainly, near §1.3 and §14.1, that monotonic decline is guaranteed by the confidence model, and relocate the genuine interest to what is *not* guaranteed — e.g. the differing slopes across group types, or which factors dominate the decline (the information-value ranking). Alternatively, introduce a mechanism under which uncertainty could plausibly *improve* a predicted outcome (e.g. optimistic priors on Unknown), which would make the demonstration non-trivial.

**2. The report reports no results.** *Location: §2.5 (poses three research questions), §6 (describes outputs), §13.1 (states purpose).* The report poses three research questions (§2.5) and describes every output the tool can render (§6.1–§6.7), but it never actually runs the instrument and reports a single number, table, or figure. There is no worked scenario, no sensitivity curve with values, no information-value ranking, not one screenshot. For a tool paper this is the central gap: the reader is asked to take the tool's behaviour on description. *Why it matters:* the reproducibility strength claimed in §9 is left entirely unexercised — the report cites the *form* of a citation ("seed 42, eight destinations, three groups, thirty per cent uncertainty," §9.2) but never presents such a result. The three research questions in §2.5 are consequently never answered in the text. *Path forward:* add a short Results section built around one or two fully specified, reproducible scenarios (the seed-based determinism makes this cheap and powerful). Show the actual sensitivity curve values for two contrasting groups, the actual information-value ranking, and at least one assignment matrix. This single addition would convert the paper from documentation into a demonstration and would let the report answer, rather than merely restate, the questions in §2.5.

**3. The authoritative evidentiary base is admittedly absent and stale, which undermines the report's provenance.** *Location: §1.7, §11.3, and pervasive reliance on "the concept note," "the methodology," and "the backlog."* Many of the report's load-bearing justificatory claims — why Willingness was promoted (§5.1), the "field taxonomy" grounding the eight population types (§5.7), the reasoning behind the 0.30 Unknown score (§5.2) — are attributed to project documents. Yet §11.3 states that the document the repository itself names as authoritative (`Evacuation_Simulator_Methodology.docx`) is *excluded from version control*, is behind the code "in at least four respects," and falsely claims to be auto-generated. I confirmed the `.gitignore` excludes `*.docx`, `*.xlsx`, and `*.csv`, so a reader cloning the repo cannot consult the source of record. *Why it matters:* a research report whose reasoning rests on documents the reader cannot see, and which the report itself says are out of date, cannot be independently audited on precisely the points where the design decisions are contestable. *Path forward:* either commit a current methodology document to the repository (removing it from `.gitignore`) or, better for this report, fold the derivations directly into the paper so it is self-contained, and clearly mark which claims were verified against code (most of §4–§5, which check out) versus which rest only on now-absent prose (much of the "why" in §5.1, §5.7).

**4. A modelling threshold is attributed to an authoritative humanitarian source it cannot come from.** *Location: §5.10, §8.2 (UNHCR 40% threshold); confirmed in `index.html` source table.* The report states the 40% minimum-readiness success floor is "cited in the tool's own source table to the UNHCR Handbook for Emergencies" (§5.10, §8.2). But "readiness" is ERUS's own composite construct (§5.5) — a weighted average of seven invented factor scores — so no external handbook can define a "40% minimum readiness threshold" for it. The tool itself concedes the floor is "an operational approximation which should be calibrated against field data" (§5.10). Attaching that invented number to UNHCR, with no page reference, dresses a modelling assumption as a sourced standard. **[Verification Required]** I could not confirm that the UNHCR *Handbook for Emergencies* (3rd ed., 2007) defines any such readiness threshold; on its face it cannot, since the readiness metric is internal to this tool. By contrast the two Sphere figures the report cites (15 L/person/day; 3.5 m² covered space, §8.2) are accurate and correctly characterised as informing *which factors exist* rather than validating weights. *Why it matters:* citation integrity is exactly what a skeptical referee tests first, and one misattributed number invites doubt about the others. *Path forward:* reclassify the 40% floor explicitly as an unsourced modelling assumption (as §8.3 already does in general terms), remove or heavily qualify the UNHCR attribution for it, and — where a source genuinely informs a parameter — give a page-level citation.

## Minor issues

- **m1. The §7.3 exclusion-rate artefact is flagged but its effect on the core demonstration is not assessed.** I confirmed in code that gatekeeper factors carry P(blocked) = 0.24 versus 0.10 for standard factors, giving P(at least one of three gatekeepers blocked) ≈ 0.56 — the "roughly five in eight" of §7.3. Since blocked gatekeepers force failure, this distribution directly shapes the very sensitivity curves that are the tool's headline. Add a sentence assessing whether the demonstration is distorted by an admittedly possibly-artefactual exclusion rate.

- **m2. No figures or tables at all.** §6 describes six distinct visual outputs; the report shows none. At minimum include one destination card, one sensitivity chart, and one information-value ranking.

- **m3. Three different 0–1 quantities share the stage without a notation table.** "Readiness," "composite score," and "success rate" (§5.5, §5.8, §5.9) are distinct constructs that a reader will conflate. A short glossary/notation box would prevent this.

- **m4. Blocked factors are assigned a base confidence (0.40–0.75, §5.3) that never enters the base score** (Blocked scores 0.0 regardless, §5.4). It does affect perturbation probability. State this explicitly so the reader does not read the Blocked confidence range as dead or contradictory.

- **m5. The repository name is a genuine reader hazard.** The Foreword handles "India Clarke, not the country" well, but naming the artefact `India-EvacSimulation` guarantees confusion in any indexed or cited form. Recommend renaming the repo (e.g. to `erus`) or adding the disambiguation to the title page, not only the Foreword.

- **m6. No licence file (§11.2) sits in tension with the reproducibility claim (§9.3).** If a reviewer is invited to regenerate and reuse results, the absence of any licence leaves reuse terms undefined. Worth a one-line acknowledgement where reproducibility is sold as a strength.

- **m7. The mobility-need blind spot is noted twice (§5.8, §12.7) but never resolved even at the level of a proposed fix.** Since the report identifies that a mobility-needs group is scored only against *medical* capacity, add the obvious one-line remedy (a separate accessibility factor, or a mobility branch in `vulnerability_match`) rather than only naming the flaw.

- **m8. References are named in prose but never formalised.** Sphere (2018), UNHCR (2007), IASC (2007), Additional Protocol I (1977), ICRC (2013), and Park & Miller (1988) all appear in §8 without full citations. Add a reference list with editions and, where a number is cited, page locators.

## Things the report gets right

- **The generate-once architecture (§4.2) is the correct design for the argument.** Freezing the scenario while the slider moves is exactly what licenses attributing outcome changes to information quality; the report understands and states this cleanly. (The caveat is Major issue 1 — the *conclusion* drawn from it is over-claimed, but the *mechanism* is sound and well explained.)

- **The Unknown-versus-Unwilling distinction (§7.2, §14.2) is a real conceptual contribution.** Relabelling a Blocked Willingness factor as "Unwilling" so the tool cannot present a settled refusal as a closable gap is precisely the kind of design choice that avoids the error the tool exists to expose. The origin story — noticing that good food scores were arithmetically outvoting a refusal — is exactly the sort of concrete design reasoning examiners reward.

- **Epistemic conservatism for Unknown (§5.2).** Setting Unknown to 0.30, deliberately below Partial, and defending it as "not treated as probably fine," is a defensible and clearly reasoned stance rather than an arbitrary default.

- **The gatekeeper cap (§5.5).** The report correctly explains why weighting alone was insufficient (a strong elsewhere-performance could still lift a blocked site above an unblocked one) and why a hard cap was needed. The cap-and-threshold consistency argument (§5.10) is a nice touch.

- **Candour as a practice (§11–§12, §14.3).** Naming the absent methodology doc, the stale published build, the missing tests, the uncalibrated thresholds, and the possibly-artefactual exclusion rate makes the instrument easier to trust about what it does claim. This is genuinely to the author's credit.

- **Prose quality.** The writing is clear, concrete, and free of throat-clearing; the plain-language framing serves the intended examiner/stakeholder audience well.

## Verdict

**Major revisions.** This is a careful, honest, well-written account of a thoughtfully designed instrument, and its correctness of description holds up against the code. It is not yet a research contribution because it over-claims what it demonstrates and shows no results. The single highest-value revision is to resolve Major issues 1 and 2 together: state explicitly that the monotonic uncertainty–outcome decline is guaranteed by the confidence model (not discovered), and then add one or two fully specified, reproducible worked scenarios whose *actual* outputs — sensitivity slopes, information-value rankings, assignments — answer the three research questions in §2.5. That move relocates the paper's interest from a tautology to the genuinely non-obvious structure the tool reveals, and finally exercises the reproducibility that is the instrument's real strength.
